Hepatitis C Virus (HCV) is a blood-borne viral infection that primarily affects the liver. It is often called a "silent disease" because many infected individuals show no symptoms for years while liver damage slowly progresses.

Panel 5 · Transmission

Who Is Most
at Risk?

Main Causes
  • Sharing needles or syringes
  • Unsafe medical or dental procedures
  • Blood transfusions (before proper screening)
  • Needle-stick injuries
  • Less commonly: sexual transmission
  • Renal dialysis patients
High-Risk Groups
  • People who inject drugs
  • Healthcare workers
  • Patients receiving frequent medical care
Key fact
HCV spreads through direct blood-to-blood contact only — it cannot spread through casual contact, hugging, sharing food, or air.

Panel 3 · Pathophysiology

How is HCV
Killing You?

Viral Replication Cycle
1
Entry
HCV attaches to the liver cell (hepatocyte) via the CD81 receptor.
2
Uncoating
The viral capsid opens and releases viral RNA (+ssRNA) inside the cell.
3
Protein Production
The virus hijacks host cell machinery to produce NS (non-structural) proteins.
4
Replication
Viral RNA is copied many times by NS5B RNA-dependent RNA polymerase.
5
Assembly
New viral RNA is packaged with proteins to form new virus particles.
6
Release
New virus particles are released from the cell to infect more hepatocytes.
Virology Detail — For Students
  • HCV is a +ssRNA enveloped virus belonging to the Flaviviridae family
  • Enters hepatocytes via surface receptors: CD81 and SR-B1
  • Extremely high mutation rate enables persistent immune evasion
  • Chronic inflammation activates hepatic stellate cells → collagen deposition → fibrosis

Panel 2 · Clinical Presentation

So What Does
It Feel Like?

Hepatitis C affects the liver — an organ responsible for detoxification, metabolism, and nutrient storage. Symptoms depend heavily on the disease stage.

🟡 Early Stage — Acute
  • Often completely asymptomatic — the "silent" phase
  • Fatigue and general tiredness
  • Mild nausea
🟠 Chronic Stage
  • Persistent, ongoing fatigue
  • Abdominal discomfort (upper right region)
🔴 Advanced Disease
  • Liver cirrhosis — irreversible scarring of liver tissue
  • Fluid buildup in the abdomen (ascites)
  • Easy bruising and unexplained bleeding
🔵 Extra-Hepatic Effects
  • Kidney problems (membranoproliferative glomerulonephritis)
  • Immune-related conditions (cryoglobulinemia)

HCV Virion — Structural Composition
🦠 Virion Architecture
🧱
Lipid Envelope
Derived from the host ER membrane during budding. 55–65 nm diameter. Surrounds the entire virion and carries the spike glycoproteins E1 and E2.
📌
Glycoproteins E1 & E2 (Spikes)
Non-covalent heterodimers embedded in the lipid bilayer. E2 binds CD81 and SR-B1 receptors on hepatocytes. High hypervariable region (HVR1) on E2 drives antigenic escape.
🔵
Nucleocapsid (Core Protein)
Protein shell encapsulating the RNA genome. Core protein also associates with host lipid droplets — critical for virion assembly and a contributor to immune evasion and steatosis.
🧬
+ssRNA Genome (~9.6 kb)
Positive-sense single-stranded RNA. Translated directly as mRNA into a single ~3,010 aa polyprotein. Flanked by highly conserved 5′UTR (contains IRES) and 3′UTR.
⚙️
Non-Structural Proteins (NS2–NS5B)
NS3/4A serine protease + helicase · NS4B membrane web · NS5A replication scaffold (Ledipasvir target) · NS5B RdRp (Sofosbuvir target)

Panel 4 · Stay Protected

Precautions
& Advice

💉
Never share needles or sharp tools
Sharing syringes, razors, or any sharp equipment is the primary transmission route. Even once is enough.
🏥
Ensure safe medical & dental practices
Only accept care at licensed facilities using sterilized, single-use instruments.
🧤
Use protective equipment in healthcare
Healthcare workers must always use gloves, masks and appropriate PPE when handling blood or body fluids.
🩸
Screen blood products carefully
All donated blood must be tested for HCV before any transfusion takes place.
⚠ Important — No Vaccine Available
Unlike Hepatitis A & B, there is currently no approved vaccine for Hepatitis C. Prevention depends entirely on safe behaviour and blood screening protocols.
✔ Key Message
Early testing and treatment can prevent severe complications and stop transmission. If you suspect exposure, get tested — a simple blood test can detect HCV, and it is now curable in over 95% of cases.
